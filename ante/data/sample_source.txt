Enzyme Kinetics (textbook excerpt)

Enzymes are biological catalysts that lower the activation energy of a reaction
without being consumed. The Michaelis-Menten model describes how reaction
velocity depends on substrate concentration. Km, the Michaelis constant, is the
substrate concentration at which the reaction proceeds at half of its maximum
velocity (Vmax). A low Km indicates high affinity between the enzyme and its
substrate, because little substrate is needed to reach half-maximal velocity.

Inhibitors change enzyme behavior in characteristic ways. A competitive
inhibitor binds the active site and competes with substrate; it raises the
apparent Km but leaves Vmax unchanged, because adding enough substrate can still
outcompete the inhibitor. A noncompetitive inhibitor binds an allosteric site,
lowering Vmax while leaving Km unchanged. Understanding these patterns lets you
read a Lineweaver-Burk plot and identify the mechanism of inhibition.

Temperature and pH affect enzyme activity. Each enzyme has an optimal pH and
temperature at which its active site holds the ideal conformation. Beyond these
optima, the enzyme denatures and loses catalytic function.
